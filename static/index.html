Introducing jerri.co:

The smarter way to monetize apps by facilitating professional profile- and permission management.

What is Jerri?

Jerri is an innovative profile and permission management tool for web- and mobile apps making per user feature activation a piece of cake. And that way allows you to monetize your apps in totally new ways.

Why do I want this again?

The App Market model is broken and doesn't pay for your development. Jerri does.

One-Time Fees

App Markets focus on one-time licence fee monetization models though your app offers an on-going-service requiring all the time service infrastructure and support.

Insufficient Privileges

You have only very little control on the usage and the priviliges in your app: either the user has it or she doesn't. Binary on-off is incapable of capturing your business model.

Broken Promotion-Model

In order to promote your paid app, you have to offer a free app to the user with either limited capabilities or content. In any case leading to having two code bases.

Missing on-boarding facilities

And then switching from the promotion-app your users can't access the content of the former app anymore, making upgrading and on-boarding a painful process with low conversions.

no monitization of Upgrades

Either you offer latest changes as app upgrades so users can migrate the data and will get informed about that, or you add as a new app so people can pay for the upgrade, but you lose all your marketing and promotion on the old app.

High fees

Payment through the app stores has very high fees and doesn't properly facilitate subscription models for on-going services and feature developments.

App markets only offer the 70th EULA-Licence-fee monetization model with all its known weaknesses. Jerri offers you a better alternative.

The One-Time-Licence-Fee-Model

  • strong new-customers-focus
  • Big-Chunks-Development to justifying another payment
  • every launch a risky gamble because of all the time invested
  • switching versions is painful and has low conversation rates

Though the advantages obviously speak for the subscription model, only few developers implement it, as it would require a lot of work on their part.

Jerri changes that.

How it works

Jerri is a hamster. Hamsters love to collect everything, especially food. Usually by putting it into their mouth and then hiding it somewhere. Well, jerri is a very special hamster, because instead of collecting food, it loves to collect user behaviour. And instead of hiding it, jerri runs a web service to collect, analyse and draw conclusions from that data. Specifically on profile and permission management.

So basically the way this works is like this:

  1. Your App starts and asks the jerri-library about the User-Permissions-Object with a var jerriUser = jerri.login()
  2. The library identifies the Device and User and looks their permission model up via the webservice
  3. Depending on the attached permission-model and already collected data, Jerri returns the permission-object to the Library

And from that point on, whenever the app needs to check whether the current user is allowed to use a certain feature in a certain way, it just calls the jerriUser.can('has_cheeseburger') and jerri will answer. Permission management made easy.

Now, Jerri offers a highly flexible range of limitation you can configure for each profile, but for this example case let's say you configured the 'Free'-Profile to be allowed thirty times and then block. Now everytime the app starts, you simply ask jerriUser.can("open_app") and if so, you log that event by saying jerriUser.did("open_app"). Then the library will push this information to the webservice and the next time it syncs (probably when the apps opens), receives the current count left.

This goes until the User opened the app 30 times, from that point on jerriUser.can("open_app") will return False and you simply show a message to the user to upgrade or quit the app. And as jerri identifies a user by its device even without a login and stores the information centrally on the webservice, reinstalling the app won't change a thing: the user can only open the App after she upgraded.

Permission management at its best.
